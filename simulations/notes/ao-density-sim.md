## Teaching Notes

This simulation builds up density plots for the wavefunctions of different atomic orbitals to show their spatial distribution around an atom. The phase of the wavefunctions are indicated by the colour of the points - blue is positive, pink is negative.

## What is an orbital?

An orbital is a mathematical function (wavefunction) that describes the behaviour of electrons, including their location around an atom. They also encode other properties of the electrons, including their momentum and energy.

The most intuitive understanding of an orbital is *where the electron is likely to be found around the atom*. It is possible to think of the orbital (or more strictly the *square of the orbital's wavefunction*) as a probability density function; the higher the value of the orbital's wavefunction, the more likely the electron will be found in that region. In terms of the simulation plots, the higher the density of points, the more likely the electron will be found there. This behaviour stems from the quantum mechanical nature of electrons: they exhibit wave-particle duality and will behave more like waves in some contexts and more like particles in others. Trying to 'find' the electron around the atom means it will behave more like a particle.

## Shapes of atomic orbitals

You will notice that the orbitals have different shapes. How these shapes vary can be broken down into two parts: a radial component (based on the distance from the nucleus) and the angular component (based on the angles around the nucleus).

### Angluar component

The angular component depends entirely on what type of orbital it is: $s$, $p$, $d$ or $f$.

s orbitals are spherically symmetric and look the same in every direction.

p orbitals have two 'lobes' on opposite sides. Between the two lobes is a plane where electrons are not found; this is known as a node (analagous to a node on a vibrating string where there is no movement). You can see from the plane slices that the three p orbitals have nodes in one of the three planes perpendicular to one of the Cartesian axes. This is where their names come from - $p_x$ has a node in the axis perpendicular to the $x$-axis.
