Why 2D

A particle box is two-dimensional, so this settles to the 2D Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution, f(v) ∝ v·e−mv²/2kT, rather than the 3D textbook form ∝ v²·e−mv²/2kT. The gold curve is the correct 2D target. What carries over exactly to 3D is everything that matters here: it rises from zero, peaks at a most probable speed, trails into a long high-speed tail, and — the real lesson — it emerges purely from random elastic collisions conserving energy and momentum, with no special starting speeds required. Temperature rescales every particle's speed live (v_rms ∝ √T), so heating slides the whole curve right and flattens it while its area stays fixed at 1 — and the box shifts from blue toward red. That growing high-speed tail is the heart of every rate-of-reaction argument. Turn on empirical average once the gas has equilibrated: it time-averages the noisy live histogram into measured data points (white) that settle onto the analytical curve as frames accumulate — with a live goodness-of-fit % — showing the analytical law isn't imposed but emerges as the long-run average of the actual particle speeds.
